# SECTION 1 — PROJECT PURPOSE AND SYSTEM UNDERSTANDING

## E-Canteen Pre-Order System: Complete Foundational Explanation

The E-Canteen Pre-Order System is a web-based food pre-ordering platform designed specifically for the school environment. It addresses a persistent and deeply rooted set of operational problems that occur daily in school canteens across virtually every educational institution. To understand why this system must exist and how it must function, it is essential to first understand the real-world pain points it is engineered to solve, the conceptual framework that drives its design, the complete workflow it orchestrates, the roles it serves, the payment infrastructure it integrates, the queue management logic it enforces, and the long-term scalability vision that governs its architecture.

### The Real-World Problems in School Canteens

School canteens operate under extreme time constraints. Break periods — whether a short morning recess of 15 minutes or a longer lunch break of 30 to 45 minutes — represent the only window during which students and teachers can purchase and consume food. During these compressed intervals, the entire school population converges on one or a small number of canteen stalls simultaneously. This creates enormous queues. A student who arrives at the canteen at the start of break may spend 10 to 15 minutes standing in line simply to place an order, then wait an additional 5 to 10 minutes for that order to be prepared. In many cases, the break period ends before the student has finished eating, or worse, before the student has even received their food. Teachers face the same problem, often foregoing meals entirely because they cannot afford to spend their limited break time waiting in a canteen line.

The root cause of these queues is a strictly sequential process that has never been modernized. In a traditional canteen, the customer must physically arrive at the stall, read the menu, decide what to order, communicate the order verbally to the seller, wait while the seller prepares or assembles the food from scratch, pay in cash, receive change, and then carry the food away. Every single one of these steps happens synchronously and in person. The seller cannot begin preparing food until the customer arrives and states their order. The customer cannot pay until the food is ready or at least acknowledged. Cash transactions require the seller to handle money, count change, and sometimes pause cooking to do so. There is no parallelism, no advance preparation, and no decoupling between ordering, payment, and fulfillment.

This sequential model produces several cascading inefficiencies. First, seller idle time before break is wasted — canteen staff often have nothing to do in the minutes before break begins because they have no visibility into what orders are coming. Then, the moment break starts, they are overwhelmed with a flood of simultaneous verbal orders. Second, order accuracy suffers because verbal communication in a noisy, crowded canteen leads to misheard orders, incorrect items, and disputes. Third, cash handling is slow and error-prone. Sellers must pause cooking to accept money, make change, and sometimes deal with customers who lack exact change. Fourth, there is no structured sales data. Canteen sellers at the end of the day have only a rough idea of what they sold, how much revenue they generated, which items were popular, and which items went unsold. There are no transaction logs, no itemized receipts, no historical trends, and no data-driven basis for decisions about inventory, pricing, or menu composition. Fifth, school administrators have no oversight of canteen operations — they cannot audit revenue, verify hygiene compliance through order records, or assess whether the canteen is meeting the needs of the student body.

These problems are not minor inconveniences. They represent systemic inefficiency that affects nutrition (students skip meals because lines are too long), academic performance (students return to class late or hungry), seller revenue (lost sales due to customers who leave the queue), and institutional operations (unstructured financial records for what is often a significant revenue stream within the school).

### The Purpose of the E-Canteen Pre-Order System

The E-Canteen Pre-Order System exists to fundamentally restructure how food is ordered, paid for, prepared, and collected in a school canteen by decoupling the ordering and payment process from the physical act of food collection. The central idea is simple but transformative: allow students and teachers to place their food orders and complete payment before break time begins, so that when break arrives, the food is already prepared or being prepared, and the customer only needs to walk to the canteen, present proof of their order, and pick up their food.

This system is built as a web-based application accessible from any device with a browser — a student's smartphone, a teacher's laptop, a tablet in a classroom, or a shared computer in a school library. It does not require the installation of a native mobile application in its initial version, though it is designed with a responsive interface that functions seamlessly on mobile screens. The choice of a web-based platform ensures the lowest barrier to adoption: any user with a browser can access the system immediately without app store downloads, device compatibility concerns, or storage constraints.

The platform allows users to browse the full menu of every canteen stall registered in the system, view item names, descriptions, prices, and images, add items to a shopping cart, select a designated pickup time slot (such as "Break 1" or "Break 2" or "Lunch"), complete payment through digital means, and then receive a confirmed order with a unique order code and QR code. The seller receives this order immediately upon payment confirmation and can begin preparing the food well before the customer physically arrives. When the customer arrives at the canteen during their selected pickup window, they present their order QR code to the cashier, who scans it to confirm the pickup. The order is marked as completed, and the transaction is closed.

This workflow eliminates the queue at the point of sale because the ordering and payment have already happened. It eliminates cash handling at the counter. It gives sellers advance notice of exactly what they need to prepare and in what quantities. It produces a complete digital record of every transaction, every item sold, every payment received, and every order fulfilled.

### Core System Workflow in Detail

The system workflow is divided into distinct phases that together form a complete order lifecycle: **Browse → Cart → Checkout → Payment → Kitchen Processing → Ready for Pickup → Pickup Confirmation → Completed**.

**Phase 1: Authentication and Access.** Every user must authenticate before interacting with the system. Students, teachers, cashiers, and sellers each log in with credentials that determine their role and the interface they see. Authentication is handled through Laravel's built-in authentication system, extended to support role-based access control. Upon login, a student or teacher sees the customer-facing storefront. A seller sees the kitchen management dashboard. A cashier sees the pickup confirmation interface. A Super Admin sees the full administrative panel.

**Phase 2: Menu Browsing.** The customer (student or teacher) lands on a storefront page that displays all active canteen stalls. Each stall has its own menu, organized by categories (e.g., "Rice Dishes," "Snacks," "Beverages," "Desserts"). Each menu item displays a name, description, price, an optional image, and an availability indicator. Items that are sold out or disabled by the seller do not appear or are clearly marked as unavailable. The customer can search for specific items, filter by category or stall, and view detailed item pages. The menu is dynamic — sellers can update it in real time, adding new items, adjusting prices, toggling availability, and uploading images through their seller dashboard.

**Phase 3: Cart Management.** When a customer selects an item, it is added to their shopping cart. The cart persists across the session and displays all selected items, their quantities, individual prices, and the total cost. The customer can adjust quantities, remove items, or add special notes (e.g., "no spice," "extra sauce") to individual items. The cart supports items from multiple stalls in a single session, though each stall's items are grouped and processed as a separate sub-order during kitchen fulfillment. The cart also displays the selected pickup time slot, which the customer must choose before proceeding to checkout.

**Phase 4: Pickup Time Selection.** Before checkout, the customer must select a pickup time slot. Pickup time slots are predefined by the school administration and correspond to official break periods — for example, "Break 1 (09:30–09:45)," "Break 2 (12:00–12:30)," or "Lunch (13:00–13:45)." Each time slot has a capacity limit to prevent the kitchen from being overwhelmed. If a time slot is full, it is displayed as unavailable, and the customer must select a different slot. The system enforces a cutoff time before each slot — for instance, orders for "Break 1" must be placed at least 15 minutes before Break 1 begins, giving the kitchen adequate preparation time.

**Phase 5: Checkout and Payment.** At checkout, the customer reviews their complete order — all items, quantities, notes, selected pickup time, and total amount. They then select a payment method. The system supports two primary payment methods:

- **Virtual Balance (E-Wallet):** Each user account has a virtual balance that can be topped up by a parent, guardian, or school administrator through a top-up mechanism. When the customer pays using virtual balance, the amount is deducted from their account instantly. This method is preferred for students who may not have access to external payment methods.

- **QRIS (Quick Response Code Indonesian Standard) / Payment Gateway:** For users who prefer to pay via bank transfer, e-wallet apps (GoPay, OVO, Dana, ShopeePay), or other digital payment methods, the system integrates with a payment gateway (such as Midtrans, Xendit, or Tripay) that generates a QRIS code or payment link. The customer scans the code or follows the link to complete the payment externally. The payment gateway sends a callback notification to the system upon successful payment, which automatically updates the order status.

Upon successful payment — whether via virtual balance or payment gateway confirmation — the order status transitions from "Pending Payment" to "Paid," and the order is immediately pushed into the kitchen queue for the respective seller.

**Phase 6: Kitchen Queue Processing.** The seller's dashboard displays all incoming paid orders, organized by pickup time slot and ordered by the time the order was placed. This creates a structured kitchen queue: orders for the earliest pickup slot appear first, and within each slot, orders are sorted by creation time (first-come, first-served). The seller can see exactly what items need to be prepared, in what quantities, and for which pickup window. As the seller begins working on an order, they mark it as "Preparing." When the food is ready, they mark it as "Ready for Pickup." This status change is reflected in real time on the customer's order tracking page, so the customer knows when their food is ready and can head to the canteen.

The kitchen queue is the operational heart of the system. It transforms the canteen from a reactive, chaotic environment — where sellers respond to a flood of verbal orders in real time — into a proactive, organized workspace where sellers have a clear, prioritized list of tasks with full information about each order before the customer ever arrives.

**Phase 7: Pickup Confirmation.** When the customer arrives at the canteen during their selected pickup time, they open their order on their device, which displays a unique QR code for that order. The cashier — a dedicated role at the canteen counter — uses a scanner or the system's camera-based scanning interface to scan the customer's QR code. The system validates the QR code, verifies that the order exists and is in "Ready for Pickup" status, and marks it as "Completed." This replaces the traditional point-of-sale interaction entirely: no verbal ordering, no cash exchange, no waiting. The customer receives their food and leaves.

If the customer arrives but the order is still in "Preparing" status, the system informs the cashier, and the customer is asked to wait briefly. If the customer does not pick up their order within a configurable time window after the pickup slot ends, the order may be marked as "Unclaimed" for reconciliation purposes.

**Phase 8: Order Completion and Records.** Once the order is marked as "Completed," the full transaction record is finalized. This includes the items ordered, quantities, prices, payment method, payment timestamp, preparation timestamps, pickup timestamp, the seller who fulfilled the order, and the cashier who confirmed the pickup. This data feeds into the reporting and analytics module.

### User Roles and Their System Interactions

The system is designed around five distinct user roles, each with a clearly defined scope of access and responsibility:

**1. Super Admin.** The Super Admin is the highest-level administrator of the entire system. This role is typically held by a school IT administrator or the system operator. The Super Admin can manage all users (create, edit, deactivate accounts for sellers, cashiers, students, and teachers), manage canteen stalls (register new stalls, assign sellers to stalls), configure system-wide settings (pickup time slots, cutoff times, order capacity limits, payment gateway credentials), view all transaction records and financial reports across all stalls, manage the virtual balance system (process top-ups, view balance histories), and perform system maintenance tasks. The Super Admin has unrestricted access to every module and every data record in the system.

**2. Canteen Seller (Penjual).** Each canteen stall is operated by one or more sellers. The seller's interface is a kitchen management dashboard focused entirely on menu management and order fulfillment. Sellers can create and manage their menu items (add, edit, delete, toggle availability, set prices, upload images, organize by category), view their incoming order queue (filtered by pickup time slot and sorted by order time), update order statuses (mark as "Preparing," "Ready for Pickup"), view their own sales reports (daily, weekly, monthly revenue; item-level sales breakdown; order volume trends), and manage their stall profile (stall name, description, operating hours, logo). Sellers do not have access to other stalls' data, user management, or system configuration.

**3. Cashier (Kasir).** The cashier role exists specifically for the pickup confirmation process. A cashier is stationed at the canteen counter and is responsible for scanning customer QR codes and confirming order pickups. The cashier interface is minimal and optimized for speed: it displays a scanning interface (camera-based or external scanner support), shows the order details upon successful scan (items, customer name, order status), and provides a single button to confirm the pickup and mark the order as "Completed." Cashiers can view a log of all pickups they have confirmed during their shift but do not have access to menu management, user management, or system settings.

**4. Student (Siswa).** Students are the primary customer group. Upon login, students see the customer storefront with full menu browsing, cart management, checkout, payment, and order tracking capabilities. Students can view their order history, check their virtual balance, and view their active orders with real-time status updates. Students cannot access any administrative, seller, or cashier functions.

**5. Teacher (Guru).** Teachers interact with the system identically to students from a functional perspective — they browse menus, place orders, pay, and pick up food. However, the teacher role is distinguished in the system for several reasons: teachers may have different pricing tiers (e.g., teacher discounts), teachers may have higher virtual balance limits, teachers may have priority pickup options, and the system's reporting module may need to segment transaction data by user type (student vs. teacher) for administrative analysis.

### Digital Payment Infrastructure

The payment system is a critical component that must be reliable, secure, and flexible. The E-Canteen system implements two complementary payment mechanisms:

**Virtual Balance (Internal E-Wallet).** Every user account in the system has an associated virtual balance, stored as a numeric value in the database. This balance can be increased through top-up operations, which can be initiated by a parent or guardian through a linked parent account or top-up portal, by the Super Admin through the admin panel (manual top-up), or through an external payment gateway top-up flow (where the user pays a specific amount via QRIS or bank transfer to add funds to their virtual balance). When a customer places an order and selects "Virtual Balance" as the payment method, the system verifies that the balance is sufficient, deducts the total order amount atomically (using database transactions to prevent race conditions), and confirms the order immediately. This method provides the fastest checkout experience and is especially suitable for students who receive a fixed daily or weekly allowance loaded onto their account.

**QRIS / Payment Gateway Integration.** For payments that flow through an external payment gateway, the system integrates with a third-party provider (Midtrans, Xendit, or Tripay are common choices in the Indonesian market). When the customer selects this payment method at checkout, the system creates a payment request to the gateway API, which returns a payment URL, QRIS image, or virtual account number. The customer completes the payment through their banking app or e-wallet app. Upon successful payment, the payment gateway sends a server-to-server callback (webhook) to the E-Canteen system's designated endpoint. The system validates the callback signature (using the gateway's secret key to prevent spoofing), verifies the payment amount and order ID, and then transitions the order status to "Paid." This triggers the order to appear in the seller's kitchen queue. The entire callback handling process must be idempotent — if the gateway sends the callback multiple times (which is common), the system must process it only once without duplicating the payment or order status change.

All payment transactions — whether virtual balance deductions or gateway-confirmed payments — are logged in a dedicated transactions table with full audit details: amount, method, timestamp, reference ID, status, and associated order ID. This ledger-style record provides the foundation for financial reporting and reconciliation.

### Kitchen Queue Management System

The kitchen queue is not merely a list of orders displayed on a screen — it is a structured, rule-driven system that ensures canteen operations remain organized even during peak demand. The queue operates on the following principles:

**Priority by Pickup Time Slot.** Orders are grouped by their selected pickup time slot. Orders for the earliest upcoming slot are displayed with the highest priority. Within each slot, orders are sorted by the time they were placed (earliest first). This ensures that orders for "Break 1" are prepared before orders for "Break 2," and within "Break 1," the first customer to order is the first to have their food prepared.

**Real-Time Status Tracking.** Each order in the queue has a visible status: "Paid" (waiting to be started), "Preparing" (currently being made), or "Ready for Pickup" (food is done and waiting for the customer). Sellers update these statuses with a single tap or click. The status is pushed in real time to the customer's order tracking view, which can use polling, server-sent events, or WebSocket connections depending on the implementation tier.

**Capacity Management.** Each pickup time slot has a configurable maximum order capacity. Once the capacity is reached, the slot becomes unavailable for new orders. This prevents a scenario where 200 students all order for Break 1 and the kitchen receives an impossible volume of orders for a 15-minute window. Capacity limits are set by the Super Admin and can be adjusted per stall if different stalls have different throughput capabilities.

**Cutoff Time Enforcement.** Each pickup time slot has a cutoff time — a deadline after which no new orders can be placed for that slot. For example, if Break 1 is at 09:30, the cutoff might be 09:15, giving the kitchen at least 15 minutes of preparation time. The cutoff duration is configurable and can vary by stall or globally.

**Order Aggregation View.** In addition to the individual order queue, the seller dashboard provides an aggregated view showing the total quantity of each menu item needed for a given pickup slot. For example: "Break 1 — Nasi Goreng ×12, Mie Ayam ×8, Es Teh ×15." This aggregation enables batch preparation, where the seller cooks 12 portions of Nasi Goreng simultaneously rather than making each order individually — a massive efficiency gain.

### Benefits for All Stakeholders

**For Students and Teachers:** The most immediate benefit is the elimination of queuing. Instead of spending 10 to 20 minutes in line, users place their order from anywhere — their classroom, the school hallway, or at home before school starts — and pick it up in under a minute. This gives them more time to eat, rest, or engage in activities during break. Order accuracy improves because orders are entered digitally with specific item selections and notes, eliminating miscommunication. Payment is instantaneous and cashless, removing the need to carry cash. Order tracking provides transparency — the customer knows exactly when their food is being prepared and when it is ready.

**For Canteen Sellers:** Sellers gain predictability. Instead of facing an unpredictable rush of verbal orders, they receive a structured, prioritized queue of orders with complete details well before the customer arrives. This enables advance preparation and batch cooking, increasing throughput and reducing waste. Sellers gain access to detailed sales analytics — which items sell the most, which time slots have the highest demand, what their daily and monthly revenue is — enabling data-driven decisions about menu composition, pricing, and inventory. Digital payment means sellers do not handle cash during the rush, reducing errors and theft risk.

**For Cashiers:** The cashier's job is streamlined to a single, efficient action: scan the QR code, confirm the pickup. There is no need to take orders, process payments, or make change. This dramatically reduces the time each customer spends at the counter.

**For School Administration:** The school gains complete visibility into canteen operations through comprehensive reports: total revenue across all stalls, transaction volumes, payment method breakdowns, peak ordering times, most popular items, and individual stall performance. This data supports decisions about canteen management, vendor selection, pricing policies, and student welfare. The digital transaction ledger replaces informal, cash-based record-keeping with a verifiable audit trail.

**For Parents and Guardians:** Parents can monitor their child's spending through the virtual balance system, top up the balance remotely, and potentially view order history. This provides financial oversight and peace of mind that the child is eating during school hours.

### Scalability and Future Evolution

The E-Canteen Pre-Order System is designed from the outset with scalability in mind, even though the initial deployment targets a single school. The architecture decisions made in the foundational version must not create barriers to future growth. The following evolution paths are anticipated:

**Multi-School Platform.** The data model supports multi-tenancy, where multiple schools can operate on the same platform instance. Each school has its own set of stalls, menus, users, and configurations, isolated from other schools. This allows the system to scale from a single-school deployment to a district-wide or regional platform.

**Mobile Application.** While the initial version is a responsive web application, the backend is designed as an API-driven architecture (using Laravel's API resources and token-based authentication) that can serve a future native mobile application (iOS/Android) without modification to the core business logic. The web frontend and mobile frontend would consume the same API endpoints.

**Integration with School Systems.** The user management system is designed to accommodate integration with existing school information systems (SIS). Student and teacher accounts could be provisioned automatically from the school's student database, linked to student ID numbers, and synchronized with class schedules (to determine which break periods a student is available for ordering).

**Advanced Analytics and AI.** The structured transaction data collected by the system forms the foundation for advanced analytics: demand forecasting (predicting how many orders each stall will receive for each time slot), personalized menu recommendations (suggesting items based on a user's order history), and waste reduction analysis (identifying items that are frequently ordered but not picked up).

**Extended Payment Options.** The payment gateway integration is abstracted behind a payment service layer, making it straightforward to add new payment providers, support international payment methods, or integrate with school-issued payment cards or NFC wristbands.

**Digital Cafeteria Ecosystem.** In its most evolved form, the system could expand beyond pre-ordering to encompass nutritional tracking (showing calorie and allergen information per item), meal plan subscriptions (weekly or monthly meal packages at discounted rates), loyalty programs (points or rewards for frequent orders), and integration with school health and wellness initiatives.

### Technology Foundation

The system is built on the **Laravel 12** framework running on **PHP 8.2+**, chosen for its mature ecosystem, robust ORM (Eloquent), built-in authentication and authorization, queue management, event broadcasting, and extensive community support. The frontend uses **Tailwind CSS** for utility-first responsive styling and may incorporate **Livewire** or **Vue.js/Inertia.js** for dynamic, reactive user interfaces. The database is **MySQL 8.0+**, selected for its reliability, widespread hosting support, and strong transactional guarantees necessary for payment processing. The application is designed to run on standard shared hosting (such as XAMPP for local development), VPS deployments, or cloud platforms (such as Laravel Forge, AWS, or DigitalOcean) with minimal configuration changes.
